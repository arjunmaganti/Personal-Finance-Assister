# **Financial Heuristics — Knowledge Base**

*Grounding document for agent system prompts. Each heuristic includes: definition, rationale, applicable conditions, failure conditions, and a correct-application example.*

---

## **1\. The 50/30/20 Rule**

**Definition:** Allocate 50% of after-tax income to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt repayment.

**Why it works:** It provides a simple, memorable framework that prevents overspending in any single category without requiring detailed budgeting. The 20% savings floor is sufficient for most median-income earners over a full career when combined with employer contributions and compound growth.

**Applicable when:**

* Income is stable and predictable month-to-month  
* Housing costs fall at or below 30% of gross income  
* No high-interest debt is present (or is being actively managed in the 20% bucket)  
* The persona is a median earner in a low-to-mid cost-of-living area

**Failure conditions:**

* In high cost-of-living (HCOL) areas (e.g., San Francisco, New York, Boston), needs alone — rent, groceries, transit — routinely consume 60–70% of take-home pay, making the 50% ceiling structurally impossible without relocating or significantly increasing income  
* For high earners, 20% savings is likely too conservative; a person earning $300k/year saving 20% accumulates faster than needed and may be under-optimizing tax-advantaged accounts  
* For very low earners, 30% toward wants may be an unaffordable luxury when basic needs already exceed the 50% ceiling

**Correct application example:** A 26-year-old earning $55,000/year after tax ($4,583/month) in a mid-cost city allocates $2,292 to rent, food, utilities, and transport; $1,375 to dining out, entertainment, and clothing; and $916 to a Roth IRA and emergency fund contributions.

## **2\. Pay Yourself First**

**Definition:** Immediately upon receiving income, transfer a predetermined savings amount to a separate account before paying any bills or discretionary expenses.

**Why it works:** It removes savings from the decision-making process entirely. Rather than saving whatever is left at the end of the month (which is typically very little), savings become a fixed, non-negotiable line item. This exploits the psychological principle of loss aversion — money you never see in your checking account is money you never miss.

**Applicable when:**

* Income is stable and arrives on a predictable schedule (bi-weekly paycheck, etc.)  
* The persona has a sufficient baseline buffer in their checking account to avoid overdrafts  
* An emergency fund of at least 1 month of expenses already exists, or is being built concurrently  
* The savings destination is accessible enough to not trigger penalties (e.g., a high-yield savings account, not a 5-year CD)

**Failure conditions:**

* For variable-income earners (gig workers, freelancers, commission-based workers), a fixed auto-transfer can trigger overdraft fees during low-income months; a percentage-based transfer (e.g., "save 15% of each deposit") is more appropriate than a fixed dollar amount  
* If the saved funds are locked in an account with withdrawal penalties or long processing times, an unexpected expense forces the persona to take on debt rather than access their own savings — defeating the purpose  
* Does not work if the persona has no buffer; the first unexpected expense unwinds the savings immediately

**Correct application example:** A 30-year-old with a $4,000/month take-home sets up an automatic $600 transfer to a high-yield savings account every payday before any bills are paid. Their bills are then paid from the remaining $3,400, and whatever is left at month-end is discretionary. The savings rate adjusts annually at performance review time.

## **3\. Emergency Fund**

**Definition:** Maintain a liquid cash reserve equivalent to 3–6 months of essential living expenses in an easily accessible account.

**Why it works:** It acts as a financial circuit breaker. Without one, any unexpected expense — a medical bill, car repair, job loss — forces the person to take on high-interest debt or liquidate invested assets at potentially unfavorable times. It is the single most important prerequisite for any other financial planning, because instability in the short term destroys long-term plans.

**Applicable when:**

* Always applicable as a prerequisite before aggressive debt paydown or investment  
* The 3-month floor applies to personas with stable employment, low fixed expenses, and dual income  
* The 6-month ceiling applies to single-income households, self-employed individuals, those in volatile industries, or those with dependents

**Failure conditions:**

* Cash held in a savings account earns well below the inflation rate in high-inflation environments, meaning the real purchasing power of the fund erodes over time; this is a known and accepted cost — the emergency fund is an insurance policy, not an investment  
* Holding more than 6 months in cash is generally suboptimal; beyond that threshold, excess cash should be invested  
* If the account is not truly liquid (e.g., a money market fund with redemption delays, or a CD), it may not be accessible during an actual emergency

**Correct application example:** A single parent with $3,000/month in essential expenses (rent, food, utilities, childcare) targets a $15,000–$18,000 emergency fund in a high-yield savings account before increasing 401(k) contributions or opening a brokerage account. Until that threshold is met, the emergency fund is the primary savings destination.

## **4\. 15% Retirement Savings**

**Definition:** Save approximately 15% of gross (pre-tax) annual income toward retirement, including any employer match contributions.

**Why it works:** Based on actuarial modeling, a person who saves 15% consistently over a 30–40 year career, invested in diversified index funds, will typically accumulate enough to replace 70–80% of their pre-retirement income — the standard target for maintaining lifestyle in retirement.

**Applicable when:**

* The persona started saving in their mid-20s and has a 35+ year runway to retirement  
* Social Security is expected to provide a meaningful supplement (typical for US-based W-2 employees)  
* The persona has no pension or other guaranteed retirement income  
* A standard retirement age (62–67) is the goal

**Failure conditions:**

* **Late starters:** A person who begins saving at 40 instead of 25 needs to save significantly more than 15% — often 25–30% — to reach the same outcome; the compounding years lost cannot be recovered at a moderate savings rate  
* **Early retirement goals (FIRE):** A person targeting retirement at 45 may need a 40–50%+ savings rate depending on their current age and expenses  
* **High Social Security recipients:** A government employee with a pension, or a high earner with expected high Social Security payments, may need less than 15% from personal savings  
* **Low earners:** 15% of a $30,000 salary is $4,500/year; this may be insufficient in absolute dollars even if the percentage is correct, particularly in high cost-of-living areas  
* **Existing assets:** A 45-year-old who has already accumulated $800,000 in retirement savings may need far less than 15% going forward

**Correct application example:** A 28-year-old earning $80,000/year contributes 10% ($8,000) to their 401(k) and receives a 5% employer match ($4,000), for a combined $12,000/year (15% of gross). They also open a Roth IRA and contribute $500/month to bring their total slightly above the 15% floor.

## **5\. Housing Costs Under 30%**

**Definition:** Keep total housing costs — rent or mortgage, property taxes, insurance, and utilities — below 30% of gross monthly income.

**Why it works:** It preserves enough income headroom for the other financial priorities. Housing is typically the largest fixed expense, and once committed to (especially via a mortgage), it is very difficult to reduce quickly. The 30% ceiling was established by the US Department of Housing and Urban Development as the threshold above which a household is considered "cost-burdened."

**Applicable when:**

* The persona lives in a low-to-mid cost-of-living market (most of the US outside of major coastal metros)  
* The persona is a homeowner with a fixed-rate mortgage (costs are stable and predictable)  
* The persona earns a median or above-median income for their area

**Failure conditions:**

* In major US cities (New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Boston, Seattle, Miami, Washington DC), median rent for a one-bedroom apartment routinely exceeds 40–50% of median local income; this rule is structurally violated by millions of people and simply describes a mathematical reality, not a behavioral failure  
* The rule was originally designed for homeowners and applies less cleanly to renters in competitive markets, where housing costs are set by market forces outside the renter's control  
* When this rule is violated, the system must not recommend that the persona simply "spend less on housing" without accounting for whether lower-cost options actually exist in their area or job market

**Correct application example:** A 32-year-old earning $90,000/year ($7,500/month gross) in Austin, TX keeps total housing costs (rent \+ utilities \+ renter's insurance) at $2,100/month (28%), leaving adequate headroom for savings and discretionary spending. If the same person lived in San Francisco earning the same salary, $2,250/month (30%) would not secure a one-bedroom apartment, and this rule would need to be flagged as inapplicable.

## **6\. Avoid High-Interest Debt**

**Definition:** Prioritize paying off high-interest debt — particularly credit card balances (typically 18–30% APR) — before directing money toward savings or investments beyond the minimum emergency fund and any employer 401(k) match.

**Why it works:** No investment reliably returns 18–30% per year. The S\&P 500 has historically averaged approximately 10% annually before inflation. Paying off a credit card charging 24% APR is mathematically equivalent to earning a guaranteed 24% return — which is unmatched by any conventional investment. Every dollar kept in a savings account earning 4–5% while carrying a 24% APR balance is a net loss of 19–20% annually.

**Applicable when:**

* The persona carries a credit card balance or other high-interest consumer debt (generally defined as APR above 7–8%)  
* The persona has already secured the full employer 401(k) match (since that is a 100% instant return and outweighs even high-interest debt payoff in priority)  
* The emergency fund minimum (1 month of expenses) exists to prevent new debt from being created by an unexpected expense

**Failure conditions:**

* This heuristic uses a strictly mathematical framework and does not account for the psychological "debt snowball" effect: some people are more motivated by paying off small balances first (regardless of interest rate) because the sense of completion sustains momentum. The mathematically optimal approach (avalanche: highest APR first) sometimes leads to abandonment if the first target takes years to eliminate  
* Does not distinguish between "bad" high-interest debt (credit cards, payday loans) and "acceptable" moderate-interest debt (student loans at 5–7%, mortgages at 6–7%), which may not warrant aggressive paydown over investing

**Correct application example:** A persona with $8,000 on a credit card at 22% APR, $15,000 in student loans at 6%, and $200/month in discretionary savings capacity should direct all $200/month to the credit card (avalanche method) until it is eliminated — approximately 4 years at minimum payments vs. under 4 years with aggressive paydown — before redirecting funds toward the student loan or taxable investing.

## **7\. Rule of 72**

**Definition:** Divide 72 by an investment's expected annual rate of return to estimate the number of years required for the investment to double in value.

**Why it works:** It gives a fast, mental-math estimate of compounding power without requiring a calculator or spreadsheet. It helps make the abstract concept of compound growth concrete and motivating. Example: at 8% annual return, money doubles every 9 years (72 ÷ 8 \= 9).

**Applicable when:**

* The investment has a relatively stable, predictable annual return (e.g., a diversified index fund with a historical average return, a bond, a CD)  
* The purpose is illustrative — helping a persona understand the long-term value of starting early or choosing a higher-return vehicle  
* Time horizon is long enough for compounding to be meaningful (10+ years)

**Failure conditions:**

* Assumes a constant, fixed rate of return — which does not exist in equity markets. A stock portfolio that averages 8% over 30 years does not grow smoothly; it has years of \+25% and years of \-35%, and sequence of returns matters enormously, especially near retirement  
* Does not account for inflation; a nominal 6% return in a 3% inflation environment is only a 3% real return, meaning the Rule of 72 overstates real purchasing power growth (real doubling time is closer to 24 years, not 12\)  
* Does not account for taxes on gains, which reduce effective returns in taxable accounts  
* Should not be used to make specific investment decisions; it is a communication and estimation tool only

**Correct application example:** To illustrate why a 22-year-old should start investing now: at an assumed 7% average annual return, their money doubles approximately every 10 years (72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10.3). $10,000 invested at 22 becomes \~$20,000 at 32, \~$40,000 at 42, \~$80,000 at 52, and \~$160,000 at 62 — without any additional contributions. The same $10,000 invested at 32 only reaches \~$80,000 by 62\.

## **8\. 401(k) Employer Match**

**Definition:** Contribute at minimum enough to your employer-sponsored 401(k) plan to capture the full employer match before directing savings elsewhere.

**Why it works:** An employer match is an immediate, guaranteed 50–100% return on the contributed dollars, depending on the match structure. A common match is "50% of contributions up to 6% of salary," meaning a person contributing 6% effectively receives 9% total — a 50% instant return before any investment growth. No other financial vehicle offers a comparable guaranteed return, which is why this takes priority even over paying down moderate-interest debt.

**Applicable when:**

* The persona's employer offers a 401(k) with a matching contribution  
* The persona is not in a financial emergency (e.g., behind on rent, facing eviction) that would make even a small payroll deduction catastrophically harmful  
* The persona is classified as a W-2 employee (this rule does not apply to the self-employed or gig workers without access to an employer plan)

**Failure conditions:**

* Many gig economy workers, freelancers, and part-time employees do not have access to an employer 401(k) match; for these personas, this heuristic is inapplicable and should be replaced with guidance on Solo 401(k), SEP-IRA, or Roth IRA options  
* Vesting schedules: some employers require 2–5 years of employment before the match is fully "owned" by the employee; for a persona likely to leave their job within 1–2 years, the effective value of the match may be lower than it appears  
* Contributing beyond the match threshold to a 401(k) may be less tax-efficient than a Roth IRA for low-income earners in a low current tax bracket

**Correct application example:** A 29-year-old earning $70,000/year whose employer matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary should contribute at least 6% ($4,200/year) to receive the full $2,100 employer match, for a total of $6,300/year — before contributing to any other savings vehicle. Stopping contributions at 3% because "the paycheck feels tight" leaves $1,050/year in free money uncaptured.

## **9\. Age-Based Asset Allocation**

**Definition:** Subtract your age from 100 (or 110 or 120 in more aggressive variants) to determine the approximate percentage of your investment portfolio that should be held in equities (stocks), with the remainder in fixed income (bonds).

**Why it works:** Stocks offer higher long-term returns but with high short-term volatility. Bonds offer lower returns but with stability. A young investor with a 40-year horizon can weather a 40% market drop because they have decades for the portfolio to recover before they need the money. A 65-year-old cannot — a crash the year before retirement can be catastrophic. This rule automates a gradual de-risking of the portfolio over time, requiring no active decision-making.

**Applicable when:**

* The persona has no other significant sources of retirement income (no pension, no rental income, no expected inheritance)  
* The persona's primary investment goal is retirement at a standard age (62–67)  
* The persona has average or below-average risk tolerance  
* The rule-of-100 variant is appropriate for very conservative investors; rule-of-110 for moderate; rule-of-120 for aggressive or early retirees with long post-retirement horizons

**Failure conditions:**

* People are living significantly longer than when this rule was formulated; a 65-year-old today has a reasonable life expectancy into their mid-80s, meaning they may need their portfolio to last 20+ years post-retirement — too conservative an allocation early in retirement can cause the portfolio to run out of money  
* Does not account for other income streams: a retiree with a pension covering 80% of their expenses can afford to take significantly more equity risk with the remainder of their portfolio, because they are not dependent on it for daily survival  
* Does not account for individual risk tolerance; a 35-year-old who cannot sleep during a market downturn and panic-sells is better off with a more conservative allocation even if the math says otherwise  
* The base number (100 vs. 110 vs. 120\) is arbitrary; the right variant depends on the persona's specific situation

**Correct application example:** A 40-year-old with no pension, moderate risk tolerance, and a standard retirement goal uses the rule-of-110: 110 \- 40 \= 70% equities, 30% bonds. By age 60, the same formula suggests 50% equities, 50% bonds — a meaningful reduction in volatility exposure in the decade before retirement. A 60-year-old with a full pension would reasonably maintain 70–80% equities since the pension already provides the "bond-like" income stability.
