# **Cognitive Biases — Knowledge Base**

*Grounding document for agent system prompts. Each bias includes: description, how it manifests in financial decisions, and how correct advice avoids or flags it.*

---

## **1\. Anchoring**

**Description:** Anchoring is the tendency to over-rely on the first piece of information encountered when making a decision. Once an "anchor" is set, all subsequent judgments are made relative to that number — even if the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant.

**How it manifests in financial decisions:**

* A person buys a stock at $80/share. It drops to $45. They refuse to sell because they are "anchored" to $80 as the stock's "real" value, waiting to break even — even though the $80 purchase price has no bearing on what the stock will do in the future  
* A person sees a jacket originally priced at $300, marked down to $150. They perceive it as a good deal because they are anchored to $300, even if $150 is still more than they should spend  
* In salary negotiation, the first number stated anchors the entire conversation; whoever names a number first gains disproportionate influence over the final outcome  
* A homebuyer anchors to the listing price of a house and evaluates all offers relative to it, rather than to independent appraisals or comparable sales

**How correct advice avoids or flags it:**

* When evaluating whether to hold or sell an investment, advice should explicitly state that the purchase price is irrelevant to the decision. The only relevant question is: "Given what I know today, is this the best use of this money going forward?" This reframe is called **forward-looking evaluation** and directly counters purchase-price anchoring  
* When evaluating a purchase or expense, advice should prompt the persona to establish an independent value benchmark before seeing a price — asking "what would I pay for this?" before looking at the sticker — rather than reasoning from a marked-down original price  
* Flag any recommendation that justifies holding a losing asset primarily on the basis of "I need to get back to what I paid for it." This is anchoring combined with loss aversion and is one of the most costly behavioral patterns in investing

## **2\. Availability Bias**

**Description:** Availability bias is the tendency to judge the likelihood of an event based on how easily an example comes to mind, rather than on actual statistical probability. Vivid, recent, or emotionally charged events feel more probable than they are because they are mentally "available."

**How it manifests in financial decisions:**

* After a major market crash (e.g., 2008, March 2020), investors panic-sell because the crash is vivid and emotionally present, even though historical data shows that staying invested through downturns consistently outperforms panic-selling and trying to re-enter the market  
* After hearing news stories about lottery winners, people overestimate the probability of winning and over-allocate money to lottery tickets relative to the actual odds  
* A person who lived through the 2008 housing crisis may permanently avoid real estate as an investment, weighting a once-in-a-generation event as though it represents typical market behavior  
* Conversely, after a prolonged bull market, investors under-estimate downside risk because losses are not mentally "available" — they become overconfident and over-concentrate in equities

**How correct advice avoids or flags it:**

* All investment recommendations should be grounded in long-run historical data, not recent performance. Advice should explicitly reference base rates: "Historically, the S\&P 500 has recovered from every correction within a multi-year window" rather than allowing recent events to dominate the framing  
* When a persona has recently experienced a financial shock (job loss, market loss, unexpected expense), flag that their current risk tolerance assessment may be temporarily skewed by availability bias and may not reflect their true long-run preferences  
* Advice should never recommend dramatic portfolio changes in response to recent news events without anchoring the recommendation in historical context. A plan built during a crash should look essentially the same as a plan built during a bull market, adjusted only for the persona's actual financial situation — not for the emotional salience of recent events

## **3\. Status Quo Bias**

**Description:** Status quo bias is the preference for the current state of affairs. The existing situation is treated as a reference point, and any change from it is perceived as a loss — even when the change would be objectively beneficial. Inaction feels safe; action feels risky.

**How it manifests in financial decisions:**

* An employee never changes their 401(k) contribution rate from the default enrollment percentage (often 3%), even though they could afford to contribute more and their employer matches up to 6% — leaving free money uncaptured year after year  
* A person keeps money in a low-yield savings account earning 0.5% APR because "that's where it has always been," despite high-yield savings accounts offering 4–5% with identical risk and FDIC insurance  
* An investor fails to rebalance their portfolio as it drifts from their target allocation (e.g., from 70/30 stocks/bonds to 85/15 after a bull run), because selling appreciated assets and buying underperformers feels counterintuitive even when it restores the intended risk level  
* A person keeps paying for subscriptions, insurance policies, or financial products they no longer need or that have better alternatives, simply because canceling or switching requires active effort

**How correct advice avoids or flags it:**

* Advice should make the cost of inaction explicit and concrete. Instead of saying "you could earn more in a high-yield account," say "keeping $20,000 in a 0.5% account instead of a 4.5% account costs you approximately $800/year in foregone interest." Quantifying inaction removes the asymmetry between action and inaction  
* When recommending a change, acknowledge that switching costs are real (time, effort, potential short-term friction) but distinguish them from the ongoing cost of staying put  
* Flag any situation where the persona's current financial setup appears to be the result of default enrollment, inherited habit, or avoidance rather than deliberate choice. These are high-probability status quo bias traps  
* Rebalancing recommendations should always include the explicit rationale that "the goal is to restore your intended risk level" — framing it as maintenance rather than a bet on the market

## **4\. Loss Aversion**

**Description:** Loss aversion is the psychological tendency to feel the pain of a loss approximately twice as intensely as the pleasure of an equivalent gain. Losing $100 feels roughly as bad as winning $200 feels good. This asymmetry causes people to make decisions that prioritize avoiding losses over acquiring gains — even when the expected value of the gain-seeking option is higher.

**How it manifests in financial decisions:**

* An investor holds a losing stock far too long because selling it would "make the loss real," even though the loss already exists on paper and selling to redeploy the capital elsewhere may be the better decision. This is sometimes called "riding losers"  
* A person avoids investing in equities entirely because the possibility of losing money feels more threatening than the certainty of inflation eroding their cash savings — which is itself a slow, invisible loss  
* A retiree keeps an overly conservative portfolio (heavy bonds and cash) well into retirement because the fear of a portfolio drop outweighs the statistical likelihood that an overly conservative allocation will run out of money before death  
* Someone with high-interest debt continues making minimum payments while also saving, because paying down the debt feels like "losing" the savings, even though the interest cost makes this mathematically self-defeating

**How correct advice avoids or flags it:**

* Reframe losses that are already incurred as sunk costs. The relevant question is always forward-looking: "What is the best decision from this point, given current reality?" Holding a losing position to avoid "locking in" a loss is acting on loss aversion, not on sound financial logic  
* When recommending equity investment for a loss-averse persona, explicitly reframe inaction as its own form of loss: "Keeping $50,000 in cash while inflation runs at 3% is a guaranteed loss of approximately $1,500/year in purchasing power." Making the invisible loss visible counteracts the asymmetry  
* For personas who express fear of market volatility, recommend automating investment decisions (auto-contributions, auto-rebalancing) to remove the moment-by-moment emotional decision point. Loss aversion is most damaging when the person is actively watching their portfolio during a downturn  
* Flag any recommendation that would have a loss-averse persona hold cash or low-yield assets far beyond their emergency fund threshold as a potential loss aversion trap rather than a sound risk management decision
